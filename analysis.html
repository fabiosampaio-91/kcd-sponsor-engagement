1.The situation & the so-what

synvert xgeeks organises KCD Porto. That organiser role is, in business terms, an asset we have not yet monetised: it has produced warm relationships with 60+ cloud-native companies, ~€59K of closed 2025 sponsorships (~€33K in 2024), and roughly 20 returning sponsors. On top of the sponsor list we also hold bulk-ticket-buyer contacts (companies like Siemens and NOS that bought blocks of tickets) and a full database of 2024–2025 speakers, many of whom work at top cloud and AI companies. Every sponsor that signed a contract also handed us the name of a budget-holding decision-maker — the person who signed.

Those companies are not a random list. They are streaming, fintech, automotive, retail-tech and product-engineering organisations running Kubernetes and cloud-native stacks — precisely the buyer xgeeks sells nearshore engineering to.

The recommendation. Run a deliberate dual-track motion. Fábio keeps securing 2026 sponsorships (Track A, organiser). In parallel — and only once a sponsorship is secure or clearly on track — a separate xgeeks business voice opens commercial conversations (Track B) across many channels: email and LinkedIn before the event, our booth and the two dinners during it, national meetups and our ongoing logistics relationship year-round. The dinners matter, but they are one door among many — the core thesis is opening doors before, during and after KCD. Measure on € of pipeline influenced over 6–12 months at a ≥4:1 pipeline-to-cost ratio — never on contact counts.

2.Plain-language glossary

A few terms are used throughout; here is what each means in one sentence.

  • ABM (account-based marketing) — instead of chasing many leads, we pick a short list of specific high-value companies and treat each as its own mini-campaign.
  • Tier — how much hands-on effort an account earns: Tier 1 = deepest 1:1 attention, Tier 2 = lighter segment treatment, Tier 3 = light-touch nurture.
  • Target — the person who signs the contract / holds the budget. We often have no direct contact with them.
  • Route-in — the warm path to the target: the sponsor's booking contact and any other relationships we've built inside that organisation.
  • "Report pipeline influenced, not lead volume" — measure the € value of real business conversations and potential deals opened, not vanity counts like contacts collected or badges scanned.
  • "Business touches slot into the gaps" — time business conversations for moments that don't clash with closing the sponsorship or running the event.

3.What xgeeks sells — the lens for reading the list

synvert xgeeks is a nearshore engineering firm (100+ engineers across Leiria, Lisboa, Viseu and the Porto region; part of Germany's synvert group since 2024). It delivers four service lines: platform engineering (Kubernetes, internal developer platforms); cloud-enabled applications (single / hybrid / multi-cloud, "DevOps 2.0"); data engineering & operations (Databricks partner); and AI-powered applications.

The delivery model is dedicated nearshore teams, team augmentation and product engineering for EU clients — senior talent stood up in weeks rather than the 3–6 months a local senior hire takes. Existing clients include Mercedes-Benz, DHL, Telekom, Wayfair and ASML; partners include CNCF, Red Hat and Databricks.

So the ideal business target inside the sponsor base is a product company or enterprise that runs cloud-native, is scaling or capacity-constrained in engineering, and has a budget-holder we can reach through a warm contact.

4.The strategic insight

The single most important idea: we hold trusted access to a room full of our exact buyers, and doors open by working that access across many touchpoints — before, during and after KCD — not at one big moment.

  • Before is the biggest window. Email, LinkedIn and our existing logistics conversation warm accounts weeks ahead. This is where most meetings are actually booked.
  • During offers many touchpoints: our booth, walking sponsors' booths, the speaker and guest dinners, hallway conversations. The dinners are one valuable asset here — not the whole plan.
  • After is where deals mature: fast personal follow-up, then year-round meetups, with opportunities closing over 6–18 months.

For high-value B2B, intimate conversations generate more qualified pipeline than the conference floor alone — a well-run executive dinner of 10–20 named accounts can influence €0.5–2M of pipeline. But the dinners only work because they sit inside a continuous, multi-channel motion that warms accounts beforehand and follows up fast afterward. And every touch stays on neutral ground: relationship-building, never selling from the stage. That neutrality is exactly what makes the access valuable.

5.The dual-track rule & the hand-off

We wear two hats with the same people: event organiser and engineering-services vendor. The plan must protect both, and the community event above all.

5.1 The rule

  • Sequence & do no harm. The sponsorship relationship comes first. Secure/protect the 2026 sponsorship, then open the business conversation — separated in time and through a different person. Never open a business approach with an account mid-negotiation on its sponsorship.
  • Never pay-to-play — either direction. Sponsoring KCD does not buy business from xgeeks, and doing business with xgeeks does not buy sponsorship perks or any program advantage. KCD is community-organised and vendor-neutral; that neutrality is the asset.
  • Separate the voices. Fábio is the sponsorship-closer / organiser and stays purely in that role — his relationship with sponsors stays clean. A different voice opens business.

5.2 Who opens the business conversation — and how

It does not have to be Fábio, and usually shouldn't be. Three concrete hand-off patterns:

7.1 Target vs. route-in

For each Tier-1/2 account we name two roles, because we rarely have a direct line to the decision-maker:

  • Target — the signatory / budget-holder. The person we ultimately want a commercial conversation with; often we have no direct contact.

Through organising KCD we've built other relationships inside these orgs — engineering managers, architects, people met at past events — and any of them can open the door. We map and use every contact we hold per account.

8.Channel playbook — the doors

The motion is multi-channel and year-round; KCD is the warm starting point, not the whole plan. Seven doors, organised by when they open.

8.1 Before the event — remote & ahead-of-time (the biggest window)

Email & LinkedIn outreach

The most important channel. Weeks ahead, the xgeeks business voice sends a peer message with a KCD hook and a light, non-salesy transition to "could we help with X". Pre-warming makes on-site meetings far more likely. Example wording is in §9.

The existing logistics relationship

Through KCD, we already talk to sponsors a lot — onboarding, info, terms & conditions, payments, invoices, booth, logistics. This is a genuinely warm, ongoing line. Once a sponsorship is closed, the business track can open off this relationship — but always through the separate business voice and never mixed into a logistics email, per the dual-track rule.

Bulk-ticket buyers

Companies such as Siemens and NOS bought blocks of tickets; those contacts are already gathered and have been spoken to. Because Fábio shouldn't run sales, he hands each contact to a sales lead (e.g. Ivan), who follows up with a KCD hook → "since your team's coming, worth a quick chat about how you scale engineering?" → exploratory call.

Past speakers (2024–2025 database)

Many past speakers work at top cloud and AI companies — KCD's exact topic. Reach out ahead of the event using the speaker database, framed around their talk and the community, leading to a business conversation only where there's real fit. The same database also helps source 2026 speakers and meetup talks.

8.2 During the event — on-site, peer-to-peer

The booth — a first-class channel

We have a booth, and sponsors are on-site all day — heads and managers are often physically present. Staff our booth with 1–2 sales + engineering managers who walk over to sponsor booths, ask about their stack and what they're building, and host the same curious conversations at ours. The goal is to get to know their engineers and understand their challenges — peer interest first, no pitch.

The speaker & guest dinners — one asset, not the plan

  • Speaker dinner — held on Day 1 or Day 2 of KCD; peers, speakers and partners; thought-leadership and relationship-building.
  • Guest dinner — invite the returning 2024/2025 sponsors we're renewing and accounts in negotiation (e.g. Kuehne + Nagel), aiming for the senior signatories or someone who can reach them.
  • How we run it: our managers — Luís Soares, André, Ivan, Gui and other xgeeks managers — simply present and work the room naturally. No assigned seating, no scripts, no planted prompts; relaxed peer conversation on neutral ground.
  • Capture lightly: after each day, jot who we spoke to, their challenge and the next step, so nothing is lost before follow-up.

8.3 Year-round & after

National meetups

We run many meetups across Portugal, from Braga to Lisbon. These are a low-pressure, year-round channel with a speaker format. Identify which sponsors can provide speakers and which want to host or have meetups, and use them to keep relationships warm between KCD editions.

Fast personal follow-up

Hot contacts hear from us within 24–48h with a personal continuation of the conversation — not a generic thank-you. Then nurture toward deals that typically close over 6–18 months.

9.Introducing the business topic — concrete wording

The framing is always peers in engineering discovering a need, not seller → buyer. The remote, ahead-of-time messages matter most. Copy-pasteable examples per setting:

Before — email / LinkedIn (most important)

"Hi [name] — great to have [company] at KCD Porto again. Outside the event hat: I'm on the engineering side at xgeeks and we do a lot of nearshore platform/cloud work. I saw you're scaling [specific thing] — would a 20-minute swap of notes before November be useful? No agenda, just peers."

Before — bulk-ticket buyer (via the sales lead)

"Hi [name] — glad your team's joining KCD Porto. Since a few of you are coming, I thought I'd reach out from the xgeeks engineering side — we help teams like yours scale delivery. Worth a quick exploratory call before the event?"

Before — past speaker

"Hi [name] — loved your KCD talk on [topic]. We work on exactly that nearshore at xgeeks. Would love to compare notes sometime — and if it's ever useful, talk about how we help teams in [their company's space]."

During — at our booth or theirs

"What are you folks building these days? … We run a lot of that nearshore — how's the platform side holding up as you scale?" Curiosity first; let the need surface before mentioning anything commercial.

During — at the dinner (a manager)

"Loved your team's talk. We pick up exactly this kind of work — happy to connect you with one of our platform leads if it's ever useful, totally exploratory."

Never. Imply that sponsoring buys business (or that business buys sponsorship perks); sell from the stage; pitch a competitor. Off-stage, peer-to-peer, vendor-neutral — always. Respect the CNCF Code of Conduct and attendee privacy.

11.Measurement

Measure this like any acquisition channel, and report in € of pipeline and revenue influenced — not contact counts, MQLs or badge scans.

  • Tier-1/Tier-2 accounts engaged — target vs. actual.
  • Qualified conversations / real needs surfaced (benchmark 5–8 per dinner).
  • Meetings booked in the 30 days post-event (benchmark 8–14).
  • Opportunities created; € pipeline influenced over a 6–12 month window.
  • Pipeline-to-cost ratio ≥ 4:1 — the number that justifies the spend.
  • Follow-up speed (≤48h on hot contacts); cost per qualified conversation.

Every contact is tagged to the KCD 2026 record so we can later report "this event opened X opportunities and Y closed-won."
